## 1. QUICK START (5-MINUTE OVERVIEW)

### What You're About to Learn
This playbook walks you through planning a 2-hour community cleanup event for 10-15 volunteers in an urban park. Based on the successful Devoe Park cleanup in the Bronx (Feb 14, 2026), this playbook covers:

- How to select a location (accessibility-first approach)
- How to recruit volunteers via social media, newsletters, and word-of-mouth
- How to coordinate supplies (Home Depot, local hardware stores)
- How to run the event day with real-time coordination
- How to keep volunteers engaged afterward (5-wave email system)
- How to scale to Cleanup #2 based on lessons learned

### Timeline at a Glance
- **Week 1:** Location scouting, accessibility audit, launch social posts
- **Weeks 2-3:** Volunteer recruitment, supply ordering, partner outreach
- **Weeks 4-5:** Event day coordination prep, final messaging, contingency planning
- **Week 6:** Execute event, capture evidence, debrief
- **Weeks 7-10:** Post-event engagement waves, planning Cleanup #2

## 2. THE 6-WEEK PLANNING TIMELINE

### WEEK 1: Location Selection & Accessibility Audit

**Days 1-2: Identify Candidate Parks**
- List 3-5 parks in your target neighborhood
- Focus on areas with documented litter/environmental concerns
- Check for accessibility (wheelchair ramps, parking, transportation)
- Visit in person if possible; note baseline condition

**Days 3-4: Accessibility Deep-Dive**
- Confirm wheelchair accessibility at main entrance
- Check for curb cuts, level ground for mobility assistance
- Identify accessible parking (street parking or lot)
- Confirm accessible public restrooms nearby
- Note any hazards (broken glass, deep holes, unstable areas)

**Days 5-7: Confirm with Partners (Optional)**
- Reach out to local park department (NYC Parks, SF Rec, etc.)
- Check if any groups are already doing cleanup
- Ask about special permits needed
- Identify community partners (school, neighborhood org, environmental group)

**Deliverable:** Accessibility checklist + 1-page location summary

### WEEK 2: Social Media & Recruitment Launch

**Days 1-3: Create Recruitment Materials**
- Write event description (50 words, action-oriented)
- Take or find park photos (before state)
- Create social media graphics (Canva template works)
- Draft newsletter announcement
- List hashtags (#ParksCleanup #CommunityAction #[YourCity])

**Days 4-5: Launch Wave A (Social Media)**
- Post on Bluesky, Mastodon, Twitter/X (if available)
- Pin post to your profile
- Share in relevant community groups
- Tag local parks/environmental organizations

**Days 6-7: Launch Wave T (Tumblr/Niche Communities)**
- Post in community, environmental, local interest communities
- Share in mutual aid, organizing communities
- Post on Reddit (r/[CityName], r/volunteering)

**Deliverable:** Recruitment posts live on 3+ platforms; baseline follow counts

### WEEK 3: Newsletter & Word-of-Mouth

**Days 1-3: Newsletter Blast**
- Send to existing mailing list (100+ subscribers ideal)
- Include event link, accessibility info, supply list
- Optional: embed signup form
- Track open rate and clicks

**Days 4-5: Word-of-Mouth Amplification**
- Ask existing volunteers to forward to friends
- Post in neighborhood Facebook groups
- Leave flyers at community centers, libraries
- Email local organizations with call for volunteers

**Days 6-7: Supply Ordering**
- Contact Home Depot, local hardware stores
- Get quotes for: trash bags (200+ count), grabber tools, work gloves, trash bins
- Confirm delivery dates
- Identify backup suppliers if needed

**Deliverable:** 10-15 volunteer sign-ups; supplies ordered/reserved

### WEEK 4: Event Day Prep & Final Messaging

**Days 1-3: Create Day-of Guides**
- Write zone descriptions (Zone A, B, C if applicable)
- Create volunteer briefing script
- Prepare info sheet with accessibility, logistics, emergency contacts
- Draft volunteer roles (Event Lead, Supply Coordinator, Zone Leads, Photographers)

**Days 4-5: Pre-Event Messaging**
- Send "1-week reminder" email to volunteers
- Confirm attendance via reply
- Share final logistics (meeting point, parking, what to wear)
- Clarify accessibility needs

**Days 6-7: Event Day Final Checks**
- Confirm supply delivery/pickup
- Verify Event Lead has first aid kit, mobility accommodations
- Brief zone leads on their responsibilities
- Test communication channels (Slack, phone, email)

**Deliverable:** All guides complete; 100% volunteer confirmation; supplies staged

### WEEK 5: Contingency Planning & Wave D Setup

**Days 1-4: Document Contingencies**
- Weather backup: select alternate date (ideally next day or same time next week)
- Low turnout: reduce zones/scope, focus on highest-impact area
- Accessibility barrier: identify accessible backup zone
- Supply shortage: identify backup hardware store
- Medical emergency: confirm first aid kit, 911 access

**Days 5-7: Wave D Social Amplification**
- Queue "today's the day!" posts for social media
- Prepare volunteer testimonial quotes (get from early sign-ups)
- Create volunteer photos/bios for social highlight
- Draft post-event announcement template (customizable with metrics)

**Deliverable:** Contingency playbook; Wave D posts queued

### WEEK 6: Execute Event & Begin Post-Event Workflow

**Day 1 (Event Day):**
- 8 AM: Event Lead confirms weather, messages volunteers
- 11 AM: Supplies arrive/staged at park
- 12 PM: Volunteers check in, roles assigned, zone briefings
- 1-2 PM: Active cleanup with real-time coordination
- 2-3 PM: Wind-down, group photo, informal celebration
- 3+ PM: Evidence collection, volunteer accountability, debrief

**Days 2-7:**
- Saturday evening (optional): Wave 1 thank-you emails
- Monday morning: Wave 2 impact report (after data compilation)
- Wednesday: Wave 3 engagement follow-up
- Next Tuesday: Wave 4 special recognition
- 2 weeks later: Wave 5 momentum for Cleanup #2

**Deliverable:** Event executed; photos, volunteer feedback, metrics collected

## 3. VOLUNTEER RECRUITMENT STRATEGIES

### Multi-Channel Approach (Proven Effective)

**Channel 1: Newsletter/Email (Best for Engaged Communities)**
- **Who:** Subscribers already interested in your work
- **Reach:** 100-500 people (engagement rate: 15-25%)
- **Effort:** Medium (1 email)
- **Result:** 2-5 sign-ups expected

*How:* Send event details 3 weeks in advance. Include accessibility info prominently. Make signup easy (link or reply-to email).

**Channel 2: Social Media (Best for Broad Discovery)**
- **Who:** Bluesky, Mastodon, Twitter communities interested in environment/community
- **Reach:** 500-2000 people (engagement rate: 5-10%)
- **Effort:** Medium (5-10 posts across platforms)
- **Result:** 1-3 sign-ups expected

*How:* Post engaging graphics with event time, location, accessibility. Include clear call-to-action. Repost 2-3 times. Engage with replies.

**Channel 3: Niche Communities (Best for Passionate Volunteers)**
- **Who:** Reddit (r/volunteering, r/[YourCity]), Tumblr communities, Discord servers
- **Reach:** 500-1000 people (engagement rate: 3-8%)
- **Effort:** Medium (research community rules, write relevant posts)
- **Result:** 1-2 sign-ups expected

*How:* Read community guidelines first. Share authentically. Answer questions. Mention accessibility. 

**Channel 4: Word-of-Mouth (Best for Quality Volunteers)**
- **Who:** Friends of volunteers, referred by organizations
- **Reach:** 50-200 people (engagement rate: 30-50%)
- **Effort:** Low (ask existing volunteers to share)
- **Result:** 3-5 sign-ups expected

*How:* Ask volunteers to forward event to 2-3 friends. Mention them in updates ("thanks for referring James!").

**Channel 5: Partnerships (Best for Scale)**
- **Who:** Local nonprofits, schools, environmental groups, neighborhood associations
- **Reach:** 100-500 people (engagement rate: 10-20%)
- **Effort:** Medium (initial outreach, then partnership updates)
- **Result:** 2-8 sign-ups expected

*How:* Email neighborhood groups. Ask if they'll promote event. Offer co-promotion.

### Event Day Contingencies

**If Accessibility Barrier Discovered**
- Event Lead pivots to accessible backup zone

- Document barrier for future venue planning

**If Medical Emergency**
- First aid kit deployed immediately
- 911 called if serious
- Incident documented in report

## 6. ACCESSIBILITY-FIRST DESIGN

### Why This Matters
Community action that excludes people based on physical ability, income, or transportation isn't really community. The Devoe Park cleanup succeeded because Alice prioritized accessibility from day one.

### Accessibility Audit Checklist

**Physical Access:**
- [ ] Wheelchair accessible entrance (ramps, no steps)
- [ ] Level ground for mobility devices
- [ ] Nearby accessible parking (street or lot)
- [ ] Seating/rest areas for people with energy limitations
- [ ] Accessible restrooms nearby

**Transportation:**
- [ ] On public transit line (subway, bus)
- [ ] Free/cheap parking for those who drive
- [ ] If far from transit, consider carpool coordination
- [ ] Clear directions including accessible routes

**Sensory Accommodations:**
- [ ] Large print materials for vision-limited participants
- [ ] Clear verbal instructions (not just written)
- [ ] Low-noise resting area (not all activities)
- [ ] Scent considerations (be mindful of heavy perfumes, etc.)

**Communication:**
- [ ] Early notice of event (3+ weeks)
- [ ] Accessibility info prominent in all communications
- [ ] Multiple ways to RSVP (email, form, text, phone)
- [ ] Pre-event check-in on specific access needs

**Supply Accessibility:**
- [ ] Grabber tools for people who can't bend
- [ ] One-handed gloves options
- [ ] Lightweight bags vs. heavy duty (choice)
- [ ] Different tool options (some prefer lighter, some need grips)

**Leadership Accessibility:**
- [ ] Event Lead is a person with disabilities if possible
- [ ] Zone leads include people with various abilities
- [ ] Alternative roles for people with energy limitations
- [ ] Photography/coordination available for those who can't walk

### Staffing for Accessibility
- Assign 1 person (ideally with disability experience) as Accessibility Lead
- Have First Aid Monitor on-site throughout
- Ensure Event Lead has training in accommodating access requests
- Brief all zone leads on accessibility (don't assume, ask)

## 7. RISK ASSESSMENT & CONTINGENCY PLANNING

### Pre-Event Risk Matrix

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Bad weather | Medium | High | Backup date confirmed; volunteers notified |
| Low turnout | Low | Medium | Scope adjusted; proceeds with fewer zones |
| Accessibility barrier | Low | High | Backup zone identified; alternative roles offered |
| Supply shortage | Low | Medium | Backup hardware store contacted; contingency orders placed |
| Volunteer no-show | Medium | Low | Workload redistributed; event proceeds |
| Medical emergency | Very low | High | First aid kit on-site; 911 access confirmed |

### Contingency Playbook

**Scenario 1: Heavy Rain (Within 24 Hours)**
- Decision Point: Friday 8 PM or Saturday 8 AM
- Action: Email all volunteers by 10 AM with new date
- New Date: Next Saturday (ideally) or Sunday same day
- Update: All social posts, site, calendar files
- Messaging: "Community first. We'll reschedule for safer conditions."

**Scenario 2: Turnout Below 5 Volunteers**
- Action: Consolidate to 1 accessible zone
- Focus: Highest-impact area or most visible part of park
- Messaging: "Sometimes big change happens in small groups."
- Evidence: Still document; adjust metrics accordingly

**Scenario 3: Transportation Issues**
- Action: Coordinate carpool from nearest transit hub
- Contact: Event Lead arranges pickup for volunteers without cars
- Backup: Offer virtual role (documentation, social media)
- Budget: Allocate small amount for gas reimbursement if possible

**Scenario 4: Supply Delivery Fails**
- Action: Immediately contact backup hardware store
- Timing: Hope for same-day or next-day pickup
- Pivot: Use cash/credit from volunteer donations if no budget
- Transparency: Tell volunteers "We have a supply backup plan"

**Scenario 5: Accessibility Barrier (Broken Accessibility Feature)**
- Action: Event Lead scouts alternate location ASAP
- Options: Adjacent park, nearby accessible location, virtual event
- Communication: Notify volunteers immediately with alternatives
- Learning: Document for future venue selection
